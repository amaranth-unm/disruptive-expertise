# The Rise of Film Editing in the Digital Age

Film editing has been a skill rising more and more every day with the rise of film production and also in a new niche job of content creation with TikTok and YouTube. The skill of editing video has been in more demand in the last several years. As film editing has become more accessible with tools like Premiere Pro, CapCut, and even iMovie, anyone can edit videos from their fingertips. This is the norm now compared to the old-school way to edit film by splicing it and taping it. According to MASV, people were playing with film from the 1890s up to the 1900s, learning to cut and tape film to make short stories. One of the most famous ones at the time was The Great Train Robbery in 1903 by Edwin Porter


## From Hollywood to the People

A quote from the book No-Budget Feature Filmmaking in the Digital Era by Adam Nixon has this line: “Artistry is freed from maleficent habitus of capital in compelling ways, perhaps permanently." This stood out to me because having this technology accessible to everyone is able to have people make their own films, with their own writers and directors having people collaborate creatively and making films that aren't controlled by Hollywood or any other big business. By simplifying the "19th-century process" of manual film splicing, people believed that putting these tools in the hands of the people would break the monopoly that large studios held over storytelling. The film we create is controlled by the people, especially film on social media because it fosters communities and creators take in critiques and comments to improve their filmmaking.

## Is Digital Editing a Blessing or a Curse?

People’s reactions to digital editing were mixed; they scaled from skepticism and occupational fear to intoxicating excitement. Many experienced editors were hesitant or even resistant, worrying about technical failures, losing the hands-on experience, the loss of quality, and the possibility of increased studio control. Some even feared being replaced or struggling to adapt, especially as jobs and workflows changed. At the same time, others were excited by the new creative freedom digital tools offered. Filmmakers like Walter Murch described an “intoxicating” ability to experiment with endless versions of a scene, while other filmmakers at Lucasfilm saw it as a way to democratize filmmaking. Overall, people were both excited and concerned by the rise of digital editing.


## The Walter Murch and Anne V. Coates Interview

In an interview between Walter Murch and Anne V. Coates, they were talking about the transition between physical film and digital film. They were talking about how whenever new technology comes out, people get high on the potential of it and overuse it in their filmmaking. The example they used was how MTV started the trend of fast cuts in editing. Now something that is taught is you can stay on a scene for more than 8 seconds, which is why in conversations between two people it always cuts back and forth between the two people. The two end up talking about it depending on who’s editing because they both believe that their editing style hasn’t changed since the switch from physical to digital editing.



## Where are we heading?

The predictions about digital filmmaking technology were largely correct, but that’s not always a good thing. Film equipment and editing software are more accessible to everyone, with our phones being a high enough quality to film. Editing software being free with your iPhone or free with CapCut gives people the ability to edit with ease; the technology to create films is available to the masses and not just for the rich. Expectations that digital editing would make filmmaking faster also proved true, as modern movies now use far more cuts than older films. However, some consequences were unexpected. The “fix it in post” mindset led to weaker production planning and relies on flashy edits over good quality substance. Additionally, while digital tools were supposed to make the process easier, they often increased pressure on editors, who now handle multiple roles like sound, color, and effects. Overall, the predictions were correct about efficiency and technological change, but they underestimated the new challenges and pressures that came with it.

## The New Tool

We see this new age of easy and accessible editing software as a tool for creativity and creation. Many people and especially businesses see AI as a replacement for this creative process of video editing. CapCut has a bunch of AI features like auto captioning, auto framing, and auto voiceover. This is how we get AI-generated videos with the same voice and how you see the same video made by several different people. I doubt AI is going to replace movies any time soon, but AI is currently in the creative film spaces like YouTube, TikTok, and even livestreaming platforms like Twitch. There are times in my own creative process of filmmaking where I do use AI, and that is as a tool to help sift through hours of footage. I don’t let it influence my choices of clips, or add effects, or even add text.
